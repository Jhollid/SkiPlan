# 3-Day House Collaboration Sprint

This is a short, high-energy plan that builds on your existing `deep-house-logic-curriculum.md`, but adapts it for three days of in-person writing with your bass-playing friend. The goal is not perfection. The goal is to have fun, capture strong ideas quickly, and leave the three days with usable song material, memorable grooves, and at least one track direction worth finishing.

You have 2-3 hours per day, so each day needs a clear mission, a light structure, and a bias toward recording instead of overthinking.

## Sprint Goals

By the end of day 3, aim to have:

- 2-3 strong house sketches
- 1 best idea developed into a more complete arrangement
- several recorded bass takes you can reuse later
- a clearer sense of what works when the two of you write together

## Collaboration Rules

Use these rules all three days:

- Start with a groove, not a discussion.
- Record early, even if the sound is rough.
- Pick roles quickly so you do not both drift into indecision.
- If something feels good, loop it and capture it before analyzing it.
- If a section is not working after 10-15 minutes, pivot rather than forcing it.
- End every session with bounced audio and clear labels.

## Suggested Roles

You:

- drums and groove direction
- Logic operation and arrangement
- keys, chords, textures, and transitions

Your friend:

- bass writing and live bass takes
- rhythmic interaction with drums
- helping judge whether the groove feels good physically

Shared:

- choosing references
- deciding which sketch has the most potential
- deciding when a part is adding value versus clutter

## What To Prepare Before Day 1

Spend 20-30 minutes before the first session getting friction out of the way.

- Create a fresh Logic project template or starter session.
- Set up one clean bass recording chain.
- Choose one drum palette only.
- Choose 2-3 references you both like.
- Make a folder for exports named by day.

Suggested references for this sprint:

- `ANOTR - Relax My Eyes`
- one groove-forward deep or minimal house track you both love
- one warmer, more musical reference with strong bass interaction

## Daily Structure

Use this structure each day.

### Part 1: Warm-Up And Reference Check

`15-20 min`

- listen to one reference
- identify one thing to steal structurally
- jam a groove immediately after listening

### Part 2: Main Creation Block

`75-100 min`

- build the main idea of the day
- record bass as early as possible
- capture multiple takes before editing too much

### Part 3: Quick Arrangement Or Review

`20-30 min`

- stretch the idea into sections
- or review all sketches and decide what survives

### Part 4: Bounce And Notes

`10 min`

- export the session
- label the file clearly
- write one sentence about what is strongest in it

## Day 1: Groove Discovery

Primary goal:

- find 2-3 grooves that feel exciting to both of you

Focus:

- chemistry
- tempo choices
- bass and drum pocket
- fast idea capture

Session plan:

1. Pick one reference and agree on a rough lane.
2. Build a simple drum loop in Logic.
3. Have your friend play bass over it immediately.
4. Record at least 3 passes, even if the first ones are rough.
5. Add only one harmonic element after the groove works.
6. Repeat this process for a second idea if time allows.

Creative constraint:

- no more than 6 core elements in any sketch

Target output:

- 2-3 short groove sketches
- at least 4-6 recorded bass takes total

What good looks like:

- the grooves feel physical
- the bass is reacting to the drums, not just filling space
- both of you can tell which sketch makes you want to keep listening

## Day 2: Turn Sketches Into Songs

Primary goal:

- choose the best sketch and turn it into a real song direction

Focus:

- section changes
- musical restraint
- live bass as a feature, not wallpaper
- defining the personality of the track

Session plan:

1. Listen back to everything from day 1.
2. Choose the best idea within 10 minutes.
3. Build a rough arrangement: intro, main groove, breakdown, return, outro.
4. Re-record or comp bass if needed.
5. Add one identity element:
   - chord stab
   - vocal chop
   - texture
   - synth hook
6. Mute anything that weakens the groove.

Creative constraint:

- every new layer must answer the question, "Does this make the groove better?"

Target output:

- 1 arranged main track draft
- 1 backup sketch kept alive in case the main one stalls

What good looks like:

- the track has sections, not just loop variations
- the bass part has intention and shape
- the arrangement creates contrast without overcrowding

## Day 3: Record, Refine, And Commit

Primary goal:

- finish the strongest collaborative demo and document what worked

Focus:

- tightening the arrangement
- keeping the best bass performance
- making the demo feel exciting enough to revisit later

Session plan:

1. Open the chosen track only.
2. Spend the first 20 minutes fixing obvious weak points:
   - timing
   - unnecessary layers
   - muddy overlap between bass and chords
3. Record any final bass overdubs or fills.
4. Improve transitions and energy flow.
5. Do a basic balance pass only.
6. Export a final demo bounce and one instrumental version if useful.

Creative constraint:

- no deep mix rabbit holes

Target output:

- 1 strongest collaborative demo
- 1 folder of saved bass recordings and sketches
- 1 short note on what to continue after your friend leaves

What good looks like:

- the demo clearly sounds like a song, not just a jam
- the live bass adds character
- you both leave with momentum instead of unfinished fragments everywhere

## Fast Decision Framework

When you get stuck, use this:

### If the groove feels weak

- strip back to drums and bass only
- try a different bass rhythm before changing sounds

### If the track feels busy

- mute one harmonic part
- remove one percussion layer

### If the section feels flat

- drop elements out instead of adding more
- automate filter, reverb, or delay for movement

### If energy drops in the room

- switch from editing to playing
- record another live take

## Best Practices For Recording Bass Quickly

- Record DI cleanly first.
- Capture a full take, then punch details only if needed.
- Keep at least one more raw take than you think you need.
- Do not over-edit the life out of a good performance.
- If timing is slightly loose but the feel is great, preserve the feel.

## End Of Each Day

Before stopping, answer:

- Which idea had the strongest emotional pull?
- Which bass part felt most alive?
- What should happen first tomorrow when the session opens?

Write the answers in the project notes or a text file so day 2 and day 3 start fast.

## After The 3 Days

When your friend leaves, fold the best collaborative track back into your longer curriculum:

- use it as your current finishing candidate
- review it with the same reference rubric from the main plan
- keep one unused sketch for future development
- note which collaboration habits you want to repeat with other musicians
